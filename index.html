Why it exists

Most WAP tooling is either inaccurate, unfinished, or painful to work with.

Waves is meant to be the practical middle ground: close enough to the original platform to be worth trusting, but modern enough to support debugging, iteration, and real development work.

Stack

Layered by design

  • GatewayKannel and the surrounding environment for compatibility testing.
  • TransportWBXML, WSP/WTP behavior, and the fetch rules that sit underneath the browser.
  • EngineThe Rust/WASM runtime that parses WML, handles navigation, and renders cards.
  • WavesThe desktop host that ties everything together into a usable browser experience.

What you can do today

Use Waves as a browser, not just a harness.

  • BrowseOpen local examples and follow linked WML pages in the same session.
  • InteractEdit fields, move focus, and submit forms through the real runtime.
  • InspectCheck state, snapshots, and timelines to understand why each transition happened.
  • RecoverLoading and failures stay visible as product states instead of dead ends.

What is shipping now

Recent work turned core browser flows into day-to-day tooling.

  • Open a deck, interact, inspect, and continue in one stable loop.
  • Diagnose slow or failed requests without losing browser context.
  • Faster navigation loops Back/history and follow-link behavior stay stable while you iterate.
  • Actionable diagnostics Transport and runtime details are visible without attaching extra tooling.
  • Local and live parity Move between local examples and live pages with the same browser shell.
  • Explicit degraded states Loading and network failure conditions are surfaced clearly, not hidden or frozen.

Proof in the product

When requests are slow or fail, the browser stays usable and explicit.

These states are now first-class behavior. You can still inspect, understand, and continue instead of guessing whether the shell is broken.
